Planar mechanics is the study of how machines move and transmit force in a two-dimensional plane. It is the analytical foundation behind robotic arms, engines, lift platforms, clamps, and most of the linkages inside everyday machines.

This course teaches that analysis through four mechanisms you return to in every lesson: the **four-bar linkage**, the **slider-crank**, the **scissor lift**, and the **toggle clamp**. Rather than meeting a new example each week, you study these four in depth, viewing each through a different question: first whether it moves, then where each link is positioned, how fast the links move, how hard they accelerate, and what forces that motion demands. Every result you work out by hand can be checked in an interactive simulator.

## What Each Lesson Answers

Each lesson applies one analytical lens to the same mechanisms. The questions build on each other.

| Lesson | The one question it answers | The skill you gain |
|--------|-----------------------------|--------------------|
| **1. [Kinematic Joints and Constraint Analysis](/education/planar-mechanics/kinematic-joints-constraint-analysis)** | Will it move, and how many motors does it need? | Count degrees of freedom, classify joints, recognise over-constraint |
| **2. [Position Analysis of Planar Linkages](/education/planar-mechanics/position-analysis-planar-linkages)** | Where is every link? | Construct positions to scale, then solve the vector loop for any configuration |
| **3. [Velocity Analysis and Instantaneous Centers](/education/planar-mechanics/velocity-analysis-instantaneous-centers)** | How fast is each part moving? | Draw velocity polygons, then confirm the velocities by differentiating the loop |
| **4. [Acceleration Analysis and Dynamic Forces](/education/planar-mechanics/acceleration-analysis-dynamic-forces)** | How fast is the motion changing, and what force does that take? | Draw acceleration polygons and find the inertia forces they create |
| **5. [Cam-Follower Systems and Motion Programming](/education/planar-mechanics/cam-follower-systems-motion-programming)** | How do I design a shape that produces a chosen motion? | Program follower motion and generate cam profiles |
| **6. [Force Analysis and Mechanism Synthesis](/education/planar-mechanics/force-analysis-mechanism-synthesis)** | What forces flow through the mechanism, and how do I design for them? | Size actuators and links, then synthesise new mechanisms |

## How the Lessons Build on Each Other

Lessons 2 to 4 share a single thread. You write a mechanism's vector loop equation once in Lesson 2, differentiate it once for velocity in Lesson 3, and differentiate it again for acceleration in Lesson 4. Lesson 6 then turns to the forces that motion requires, and to the reverse problem of designing a mechanism to meet a specification. Lesson 5 covers the one case where you design the motion directly: the cam profile.

Because the mechanisms stay the same, concepts are introduced once and reused. Lesson 1 defines the mechanisms and their joints; later lessons refer back to them instead of starting over.

## How Each Lesson Is Structured

<Steps>
1. **Real-world system problem**
   A working machine that depends on the lesson's analysis, with the specific question it raises.

2. **Fundamental theory**
   The mathematics and kinematics you need, derived from first principles.

3. **Worked applications, drawn first**
   Each problem is solved graphically before it is solved with equations: you draw the space diagram to scale, then the velocity diagram, then the acceleration diagram, and measure the answers off the page. The closed-form calculation and the simulator then confirm what you drew.

4. **Design guidelines**
   Practical rules for using the analysis in your own designs.
</Steps>

:::note[What you will need]
Keep a **drawing set** beside you: set squares, a compass, a protractor, and a scale rule. Every worked example is constructed to scale by hand first, because drawing the diagrams yourself builds the spatial intuition that the equations and simulators only confirm, and it is direct preparation for sketching the same mechanisms in [parametric CAD with FreeCAD](/education/parametric-mechanical-cad-freecad/). The reference figure in each Application is the answer key to check your own drawing against.
:::

The four mechanisms here (the four-bar, slider-crank, scissor lift, and toggle clamp, plus the cam of Lesson 5) are the same ones you build parametrically in the [Parametric Mechanical CAD with FreeCAD](/education/parametric-mechanical-cad-freecad/) course. The kinematics you learn here (loop closure, extreme positions, velocity and acceleration) is exactly what sets the dimensions and constraints of those CAD models, so the two courses reinforce each other: analyse the motion here, then draw and dimension the mechanism there.

## Prerequisites

Vector mechanics, basic calculus, and elementary physics. The graphical work needs only a drawing set; the analysis scripts use Python with NumPy and Matplotlib, so a working knowledge of Python is helpful but not required to follow the worked solutions.

## Key Resources

- **Interactive simulators and labs:** the four simulators above and the [Mechanism Design and Simulation](/education/mechanism-design-simulation/) experiments
- **Programming:** Python with NumPy and Matplotlib for the analysis scripts
- **Textbooks:** *Theory of Machines and Mechanisms* (Uicker, Pennock and Shigley), *Kinematics and Dynamics of Machinery* (Wilson and Sadler)

Start with [Lesson 1: Kinematic Joints and Constraint Analysis](/education/planar-mechanics/kinematic-joints-constraint-analysis) to learn whether a mechanism moves before you analyse how it moves.
